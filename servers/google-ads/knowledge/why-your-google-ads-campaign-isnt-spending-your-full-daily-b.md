## Key Tactics

- **Check impression share metrics** to diagnose underspend issues. If search loss due to rank is high (75%+) while search loss due to budget is 0%, your bid strategy is the limiting factor, not your budget.

- **Recognize that aggressive ROAS targets create bid ceilings.** When a target ROAS is set very high (e.g., 900%), Google will reduce bids to hit that profitability goal, which prevents full budget spend even if you're willing to allocate more.

- **Review your bid strategy's performance against its goal.** If your bid strategy is hitting its target exactly (e.g., 900% ROAS at 900% target), it's working as designed but may be artificially capping your spending potential.

- **Lower your ROAS target or adjust bid adjustments if you want to increase spend.** If budget capacity exists but bids are preventing spend, you need to give Google more room to bid by relaxing profitability requirements.

- **Monitor the gap between daily budget allocation and actual daily spend.** A persistent gap (e.g., $500 budget but only $100-200 daily spend) signals a bid constraint rather than a budget constraint.

## Summary
When a Google Ads campaign isn't spending its full daily budget, the issue is often not insufficient budget allocation but rather an overly aggressive bid strategy or ROAS target that caps how much Google can spend while meeting your profitability goals. Use impression share and search loss metrics to confirm that rank-based losses are the bottleneck, then adjust your bid strategy to increase spending capacity.
